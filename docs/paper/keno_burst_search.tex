\begin{abstract}
Keno is a research software prototype for searching short gravitational-wave
bursts without assuming a known waveform shape. A neural network learns to
predict detector noise from whitened sensor data; that prediction is
subtracted; and the leftover residual is searched with an energy-based
statistic, a two-detector timing check, and a simple consistency veto.

On synthetic bursts injected into real LIGO noise, at a 1\% false-alarm rate
(about one false trigger per hundred noise-only tests), Keno residual search
reaches near 100\% detection efficiency from signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)\,2
to\,12. Searching the same data without subtraction, or with a deliberately
wrong matched-filter template, is less efficient. On published event times
from public catalogs, a coherent two-detector residual search recovers 9 of
18 dual-detector events after rejecting a known glitch near GW170817.

Keno is meant to complement morphology-specific binary black hole classifiers,
not replace them. The contribution is a reproducible software path for
unmodeled residual search.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
 
LIGO and Virgo record a time series called \emph{strain}: a dimensionless
measure of how much spacetime stretches as gravitational waves pass the
detector. In software terms, strain is a 1D signal sampled at high rate
(here $4096\,\mathrm{Hz}$). Before analysis it is usually \emph{whitened}:
filtered so that stationary background noise has roughly unit variance across
frequency, which makes short anomalies easier to see.

Search methods fall into two complementary families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Morphology-specific / template-based.} If the expected signal
        shape is known (for example a binary black hole merger chirp), one can
        match-filter against templates or train a classifier on that family.
        AresGW~\citep{nousi2023,koloniari2025} is a successful example of the
        classifier approach for black hole mergers.
  \item \textbf{Template-free / unmodeled.} If the shape is unknown (short
        rings, noise-like bursts, or other transients), one instead looks for
        localized excess energy that is consistent across detectors, without
        assuming a particular waveform.
\end{itemize}

Keno belongs to the second family. It adds a generative denoising step before
the energy search: a neural network predicts instrumental noise $\hat N$ and
forms a cleaned residual
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{clean}} = S_{\mathrm{raw}} - \hat N.
\end{equation}
The residual is then searched with excess power (an STFT peak-energy
statistic), an H1+L1 lag scan (Hanford and Livingston detectors), and an
envelope-consistency veto that rejects loud single-detector artifacts
(\emph{glitches}).

Keno was developed as an independent research prototype by a software engineer.
It is not a LIGO collaboration product and is not presented as a production
search pipeline.

\paragraph{Relation to morphology-specific classifiers.}
Systems such as AresGW~\citep{nousi2023} answer: ``does this segment look like
a black hole merger I was trained on?'' Keno answers: ``after I remove the
noise I think I understand, does excess energy remain?'' Those are different
jobs. Keno is intended as a complementary contribution for unmodeled bursts.

\paragraph{Claim.}
In this prototype, generative subtraction followed by residual excess-power
search improves recovery of unknown-morphology bursts at controlled false-alarm
rate, relative to (i)~excess power on the unsubtracted strain and
(ii)~matched filtering with a deliberately wrong template.

\section{Background terms (for software readers)}

\begin{description}
  \item[SNR] Signal-to-noise ratio. Roughly how loud the injected burst is
        relative to the background; higher SNR is easier to detect.
  \item[FAR] False-alarm rate. The fraction of pure-noise trials that still
        cross the detection threshold. A 1\% FAR means about 1 in 100
        noise-only windows is counted as a false trigger.
  \item[Matched filter] Correlate the data against a known waveform template.
        Very powerful when the template matches the true signal; weak when it
        does not.
  \item[Excess power] A template-free energy statistic: look for a loud
        time--frequency patch relative to a robust noise floor (here, the
        median STFT power).
  \item[Residual] What remains after subtracting predicted noise from the
        input. Ideally background shrinks and a real burst survives.
  \item[Glitch] A non-astrophysical detector artifact that can look like a
        burst in one interferometer.
  \item[H1 / L1] LIGO Hanford and LIGO Livingston. Requiring both reduces
        single-detector false alarms.
\end{description}

\subsection{Generative noise model}
Keno uses a 1D U-Net~\citep{ronneberger2015} with four encoder/decoder stages
(base width 16 channels, kernel size 9, GELU activations, batch normalization).
In software terms this is a convolutional autoencoder-style network for 1D
sequences: it maps whitened strain $S_{\mathrm{raw}}$ to a predicted noise
waveform $\hat N$. The residual is
\begin{equation}
R = S_{\mathrm{raw}} - \hat N.
\end{equation}
Training uses cached real public LIGO background windows (GWOSC) for H1/L1.
A residual loss term encourages injected short bursts to survive subtraction
instead of being absorbed into $\hat N$. A hold-out acceptance check requires
mean normalized recovery error
$\mathrm{RMS}(R-s)/\mathrm{RMS}(s)<0.15$ on injections with known ground-truth
signal $s$. The checkpoint used here is frozen as
\texttt{2026-07-paper-v1} (SHA256 prefix \texttt{55ce7637\ldots}).

\subsection{Template-free excess-power statistic}
For a residual or raw series $x(t)$, Keno computes a short-time Fourier
transform (Hann window, $n_{\mathrm{perseg}}=256$, 75\% overlap) --- the usual
STFT used in audio/DSP work. It forms bin power $P(f,t)$, estimates a robust
noise floor as $\mathrm{median}(P)$, and defines
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(x)
  = \max_{f,t}\frac{P(f,t)}{\mathrm{median}(P)}.
\end{equation}
Intuitively: ``how many times louder is the loudest spectrogram cell than the
typical background cell?'' No template correlation is required.

\subsection{Single-detector residual gate}
A residual threshold $\theta_{\mathrm{IFO}}$ is calibrated on noise-only
windows at a target FAR. Because imperfect subtraction can create rare large
artifacts, the upper 1\% of noise-only residual scores is trimmed before
taking the $(1-\mathrm{FAR})$ percentile. In the freeze,
$\theta_{\mathrm{IFO}}\approx 4004.6$ at 1\% FAR. This gate is useful for
single-detector diagnostics. Dual-detector production detection uses the
coherent threshold below.

\subsection{Coherent dual-detector lag scan}
For residuals from Hanford and Livingston, $R_{\mathrm{H}}$ and
$R_{\mathrm{L}}$, Keno scans small time shifts
$|\ell|/f_s \le 10\,\mathrm{ms}$ (about the light-travel time between sites)
and relative sign flips $p\in\{+1,-1\}$:
\begin{equation}
C_{\ell,p}
  = \frac{R_{\mathrm{H}} + p\,\mathcal{S}_{\ell}(R_{\mathrm{L}})}{\sqrt{2}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{S}_{\ell}$ is a zero-padded shift. The coherent statistic is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{coh}}
  = \max_{\ell,p}\,\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(C_{\ell,p}),
\end{equation}
with best lag $\tau^{\star}=\ell^{\star}/f_s$. This is a software
cross-check: a real astrophysical signal should line up in both detectors
within a few milliseconds; a random glitch usually will not.

\subsection{Envelope-consistency veto}
Independent residual envelope peak times $t_{\mathrm{H}}$ and $t_{\mathrm{L}}$
are estimated from a short smoothed energy envelope (8\,ms boxcar), ignoring
the first/last 64\,ms so edge artifacts do not dominate. Define
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{env}} = (t_{\mathrm{L}}-t_{\mathrm{H}})\times 10^{3}
\quad\text{(ms)}.
\end{equation}
If $|\Delta t_{\mathrm{env}}| > 50\,\mathrm{ms}$ while the coherent score is
loud, the candidate is treated as likely single-detector glitch contamination
(for example the known Livingston glitch near GW170817).

\subsection{Production detection rule}
A dual-detector production trigger is declared if and only if
\begin{align}
\rho_{\mathrm{coh}} &\ge \theta_{\mathrm{coh}}, \\
|\tau^{\star}| &\le 10\,\mathrm{ms}, \\
|\Delta t_{\mathrm{env}}| &\le 50\,\mathrm{ms}.
\end{align}
The coherent threshold $\theta_{\mathrm{coh}}$ is calibrated on dual-detector
noise trials that already pass the envelope gate. With 1500 noise trials
(23 envelope-gated), the 99th percentile of gated coherent EP yields
$\theta_{\mathrm{coh}}\approx 173.1$ at empirical joint FAR $0.07\%$
(1/1500). After gating, coherent noise is much quieter than the single-detector
residual threshold.

\subsection{Baselines}
All baselines use the same whitened windows and the same target FAR on
noise-only trials:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Oracle matched filter} --- correlate against the true injected
        waveform (injection studies only; an upper bound).
  \item \textbf{Mismatched matched filter} --- correlate against a fixed
        sine-Gaussian template that does not match the injected family
        (control for wrong-template search).
  \item \textbf{Raw excess power} --- $\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(S_{\mathrm{raw}})$
        with no generative subtraction.
  \item \textbf{BBH-trained ResNet (control)} --- a compact 1D ResNet trained
        only on black-hole-merger-like chirps versus noise in the same cache,
        scored as $P(\mathrm{signal})$ on the central 1\,s. This is a local
        morphology-specific control in the spirit of BBH classifiers such as
        AresGW~\citep{nousi2023}; it is not a published external weight file.
  \item \textbf{Keno residual EP} --- $\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(R)$.
  \item \textbf{Keno overlap} --- cosine similarity between residual and
        injected signal (evaluation only; needs ground truth).
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiments}

Injection campaigns mix short unknown morphologies (sine-Gaussian, ringdown,
white-noise burst) into cached real LIGO background at equal FAR.
Additional studies include a glitch stress test on public O3 Gravity Spy
labels, dual-detector noise coincidence FAR, and a follow-up on published
GWTC/cWB GPS times (public catalog timestamps). The catalog follow-up is a
consistency check, not a discovery claim.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Unknown-morphology efficiency}
Figure~\ref{fig:eff} shows Keno residual EP at 100\% efficiency across
SNR\,2--12 at 1\% FAR. Raw excess power on unsubtracted strain is less
efficient on the same injections. Mismatched matched filtering remains near
$\sim$20\% and never reaches 50\% efficiency. The BBH-trained ResNet control,
evaluated on these non-BBH bursts, plateaus below saturation --- expected for
a morphology-specific model outside its training objective.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{figures/fig2_efficiency.png}
  \caption{Detection efficiency vs injected SNR at 1\% FAR on unknown morphology.
  Baselines include raw excess power, mismatched matched filtering, and a
  BBH-trained ResNet control evaluated off-template.}
  \label{fig:eff}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Coherent calibration}
Envelope gating removes most dual-detector noise outliers. With 1500 noise
trials (23 envelope-gated), the coherent EP threshold is $173.1$ at empirical
joint FAR $0.07\%$, separate from the single-detector residual threshold
$\approx 4005$.

\subsection{Catalog GPS follow-up}
Production coincidence recovers 9/18 dual-detector catalog events
(Figure~\ref{fig:followup}). GW170817 is rejected by the envelope veto despite
huge coherent EP (Livingston glitch). Contaminated recoveries are not counted
as detections. A small set of cWB-only candidates remains 0/3.

\section{Discussion and limitations}

Keno is proposed as a complementary software path for unmodeled residual
search. Morphology-specific BBH classifiers and matched-filter compact-binary
pipelines~\citep{nousi2023,koloniari2025} remain the right tools when the
target family is known.

Known limitations of this prototype include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item weak single-detector glitch rejection; coincidence and the envelope
        veto do most of that work;
  \item residual over-subtraction risk if training is insufficient, mitigated
        by the recovery-error acceptance check;
  \item incomplete recovery of some catalog candidates in this freeze;
  \item development by a single engineer with a research codebase, without
        collaboration-scale detector characterization or production operations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

Generative noise subtraction followed by template-free residual search can
recover unknown-morphology bursts at controlled false-alarm rate in a
reproducible research prototype. Keno is released as a software contribution
for unmodeled residual search --- complementary to existing BBH classifiers ---
for audit and extension, not as a finished gravitational-wave pipeline.
