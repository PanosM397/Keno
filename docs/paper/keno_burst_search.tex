\begin{abstract}
Keno is a research prototype for detecting short gravitational-wave bursts
without assuming a known waveform shape. A U-Net predicts instrumental noise
from whitened strain; after subtraction, the residual is searched with an STFT
excess-power statistic, a Hanford--Livingston lag scan, and an envelope-timing
veto.

The implementation is end to end: PyTorch/FastAPI inference, a Node.js
orchestration layer with in-memory caching, and an Angular interface for
synchronized inspection of raw strain, predicted noise, and residuals.

On synthetic bursts injected into real LIGO background, Keno reaches near
100\% detection efficiency from SNR\,2 to\,12 at 1\% false-alarm rate (FAR).
Raw excess power on unsubtracted strain, and matched filtering with a
deliberately mismatched template, achieve lower efficiency on the same
injections. On 18 published dual-detector catalog times, the coherent residual
search recovers 9 events; GW170817 is rejected by the envelope veto owing to a
known Livingston glitch.

Keno is intended to complement morphology-specific binary black hole
classifiers such as AresGW, not to replace them. The release bundles model,
API, and inspection tooling for reproducible unmodeled residual search.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

LIGO and Virgo record \emph{strain}: a dimensionless measure of spacetime
stretch induced by passing gravitational waves. In analysis pipelines this
appears as a high-rate one-dimensional time series; here the sampling rate is
$4096\,\mathrm{Hz}$. Data are typically \emph{whitened} first---filtered so
that stationary background noise has approximately unit variance across
frequency, which improves sensitivity to short transients.

Search methods fall into two complementary families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Template-based / morphology-specific.} When the expected signal
        shape is known (for example a binary black hole merger chirp), one may
        match-filter against templates or train a classifier on that family.
        AresGW~\citep{nousi2023,koloniari2025} exemplifies the classifier
        approach for mergers.
  \item \textbf{Template-free / unmodeled.} When the morphology is unknown
        (short ringdowns, noise-like bursts, or other transients), the search
        targets localized excess energy consistent across detectors, without
        assuming a particular waveform.
\end{itemize}

Keno belongs to the second family. A neural network estimates instrumental
noise $\hat{N}$ and forms the residual
\begin{equation}
R = S_{\mathrm{raw}} - \hat{N}.
\end{equation}
Throughout this paper, $R$ denotes that residual (sometimes referred to
informally as ``cleaned strain''). Detection proceeds via excess power---peak
STFT energy relative to a robust noise floor---followed by an H1+L1 lag scan
and an envelope-consistency veto to reject loud single-detector glitches.

Keno was developed as an independent research prototype and is not a LIGO
collaboration product. It should not be interpreted as a production search
pipeline. The repository includes model weights together with ingestion,
inference, evaluation scripts, and a browser-based viewer for residual
inspection.

\paragraph{Relation to morphology-specific classifiers.}
Morphology-specific systems such as AresGW~\citep{nousi2023} test whether a
segment resembles a black-hole merger seen in training. Keno tests whether
excess energy remains after subtracting the estimated noise component. These
address distinct detection problems; Keno is positioned as a complementary tool
for unmodeled bursts.

\paragraph{Scope of claims.}
In this prototype, generative subtraction followed by residual excess-power
search improves recovery of unknown-morphology injections at fixed FAR,
relative to (i)~the same statistic applied to raw strain and (ii)~matched
filtering with a deliberately mismatched sine-Gaussian template.

\section{Terminology}

The following definitions are provided for readers less familiar with
gravitational-wave search terminology.

\begin{description}
  \item[SNR] Signal-to-noise ratio. Higher values correspond to louder
        injections relative to background.
  \item[FAR] False-alarm rate: the fraction of pure-noise trials that exceed
        the detection threshold. At 1\% FAR, approximately one noise-only
        window in one hundred yields a false trigger.
  \item[Matched filter] Cross-correlation of the data with a known waveform
        template. Effective when the template matches the signal; weak
        otherwise.
  \item[Excess power] A template-free energy statistic: the loudest
        time--frequency cell in an STFT, normalized by a robust noise floor
        (here, median STFT power).
  \item[Residual $R$] $S_{\mathrm{raw}} - \hat{N}$. Background energy should
        decrease; a genuine burst should largely survive.
  \item[Glitch] A non-astrophysical detector artifact. Gravity Spy catalogs
        common morphological classes (Blip, Koi Fish, Scattered Light, and
        others).
  \item[H1 / L1] LIGO Hanford and Livingston. Requiring both detectors reduces
        single-detector false alarms.
  \item[GWOSC] Gravitational Wave Open Science Center, the public HTTP archive
        from which Keno retrieves open strain frames.
\end{description}

\subsection{Software stack}
Keno is implemented as three coordinated services.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{ML engine (Python / FastAPI / PyTorch).}
        Loads the U-Net checkpoint, fetches and whitens strain via
        \texttt{gwpy}, performs subtraction, and exposes REST endpoints for
        denoising (\texttt{POST /api/v1/denoise}), single-detector detection
        (\texttt{POST /api/v1/detect}), and dual-detector coincidence
        (\texttt{POST /api/v1/detect/coincidence}). Offline evaluation modules
        (\texttt{app.prove}, injection campaigns, glitch stress tests, catalog
        follow-up) invoke the same residual-search logic as the live API.
  \item \textbf{Backend orchestrator (Node.js / Express).}
        Proxies the Angular frontend to the ML engine, forwards GWOSC and
        offline error details, and caches denoise/detect responses in memory.
        Initial GWOSC downloads may require 1--3 minutes; inference requests
        therefore use a timeout of $300\,\mathrm{s}$, whereas health checks use
        $5\,\mathrm{s}$. Repeated queries for the same GPS window typically
        return from cache within seconds.
  \item \textbf{Frontend (Angular).}
        Displays raw strain, predicted noise $\hat{N}$, and residual $R$ on
        synchronized axes for preset catalog events (e.g.\ GW150914) or
        user-specified GPS times. The interface also reports blind-search
        quantities: raw versus residual excess power, fraction of threshold,
        and H1+L1 coincidence flags. A synthetic validation mode injects a
        known burst to verify the subtraction path without a live download.
\end{itemize}

GWOSC ingestion is centralized in the ML service: frames surrounding a GPS
timestamp are fetched, validated for NaN gaps on offline detectors, whitened,
and returned as arrays. Cached background segments used for training and FAR
calibration are stored on disk so that evaluation campaigns avoid redundant
downloads. The architecture supports identical residual processing from either
the web interface or command-line evaluation scripts.

\subsection{Generative noise model}
The noise model is a 1D U-Net~\citep{ronneberger2015} with four encoder/decoder
stages (16 base channels, kernel size 9, GELU activations, batch
normalization). It maps whitened strain $S_{\mathrm{raw}}$ to a predicted noise
waveform $\hat{N}$, yielding
\begin{equation}
R = S_{\mathrm{raw}} - \hat{N}.
\end{equation}
Training uses cached real H1/L1 background windows from GWOSC. A residual loss
term encourages injected short bursts to survive subtraction rather than being
absorbed into $\hat{N}$. A hold-out acceptance criterion requires mean
normalized recovery error $\mathrm{RMS}(R-s)/\mathrm{RMS}(s)<0.15$ on
injections with known ground-truth signal $s$. The checkpoint used in this
study is frozen as \texttt{2026-07-paper-v1} (SHA256 prefix
\texttt{55ce7637\ldots}).

\subsection{Excess-power statistic}
For a residual or raw series $x(t)$, Keno computes a short-time Fourier
transform (Hann window, $n_{\mathrm{perseg}}=256$, 75\% overlap), forms bin
power $P(f,t)$, estimates the noise floor as $\mathrm{median}(P)$, and defines
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(x)
  = \max_{f,t}\frac{P(f,t)}{\mathrm{median}(P)}.
\end{equation}
This quantity measures how much the brightest spectrogram cell exceeds typical
background; no template correlation is required.

\subsection{Single-detector residual gate}
The residual threshold $\theta_{\mathrm{IFO}}$ is calibrated on noise-only
windows at the target FAR. Because imperfect subtraction can produce occasional
large artifacts, the upper 1\% of noise-only residual scores is trimmed before
computing the $(1-\mathrm{FAR})$ percentile. In the frozen configuration,
$\theta_{\mathrm{IFO}}\approx 4004.6$ at 1\% FAR. Dual-detector production
detection uses the coherent threshold below; this gate serves primarily for
single-detector diagnostics.

\subsection{Coherent dual-detector lag scan}
Given Hanford and Livingston residuals $R_{\mathrm{H}}$ and $R_{\mathrm{L}}$,
Keno scans time shifts with $|\ell|/f_s \le 10\,\mathrm{ms}$ (comparable to the
inter-site light-travel time) and relative sign flips $p\in\{+1,-1\}$:
\begin{equation}
C_{\ell,p}
  = \frac{R_{\mathrm{H}} + p\,\mathcal{S}_{\ell}(R_{\mathrm{L}})}{\sqrt{2}},
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{S}_{\ell}$ denotes zero-padded shifting. The coherent statistic
is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{coh}}
  = \max_{\ell,p}\,\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(C_{\ell,p}),
\end{equation}
with best lag $\tau^{\star}=\ell^{\star}/f_s$. Astrophysical signals are
expected to align within a few milliseconds across detectors; most glitches do
not.

\subsection{Envelope-consistency veto}
Envelope peak times $t_{\mathrm{H}}$ and $t_{\mathrm{L}}$ are estimated from
a smoothed energy envelope (8\,ms boxcar), excluding the first and last
64\,ms to limit edge effects. Define
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_{\mathrm{env}} = (t_{\mathrm{L}}-t_{\mathrm{H}})\times 10^{3}
\quad\text{(ms)}.
\end{equation}
When $|\Delta t_{\mathrm{env}}| > 50\,\mathrm{ms}$ and the coherent score
remains high, the candidate is classified as likely single-detector
contamination, as observed for the Livingston glitch near GW170817.

\subsection{Production detection rule}
A dual-detector production trigger is declared when
\begin{align}
\rho_{\mathrm{coh}} &\ge \theta_{\mathrm{coh}}, \\
|\tau^{\star}| &\le 10\,\mathrm{ms}, \\
|\Delta t_{\mathrm{env}}| &\le 50\,\mathrm{ms}.
\end{align}
The coherent threshold $\theta_{\mathrm{coh}}$ is calibrated on dual-detector
noise trials that satisfy the envelope gate. From 1500 noise trials (23
envelope-gated), the 99th percentile of gated coherent EP yields
$\theta_{\mathrm{coh}}\approx 173.1$, corresponding to empirical joint FAR
$0.07\%$ (1/1500). After gating, coherent noise scores lie well below the
single-detector residual threshold.

\subsection{Baselines}
All baselines use the same whitened windows and the same target FAR on
noise-only trials:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Oracle matched filter} --- correlation against the true
        injected waveform (injection studies only; upper bound).
  \item \textbf{Mismatched matched filter} --- correlation against a fixed
        sine-Gaussian template that does not match the injected family.
  \item \textbf{Raw excess power} --- $\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(S_{\mathrm{raw}})$
        without generative subtraction.
  \item \textbf{BBH-trained ResNet (control)} --- a compact 1D ResNet trained
        on merger-like chirps versus noise in the same cache, scored as
        $P(\mathrm{signal})$ on the central 1\,s. This local
        morphology-specific control follows the design of AresGW~\citep{nousi2023}
        but is not an external published checkpoint.
  \item \textbf{Keno residual EP} --- $\rho_{\mathrm{EP}}(R)$.
  \item \textbf{Keno overlap} --- cosine similarity between residual and
        injected signal (evaluation only; requires ground truth).
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiments}

Injection campaigns embed sine-Gaussian, ringdown, and white-noise bursts into
cached real LIGO background at equal FAR. Additional studies include a glitch
stress test on public O3 Gravity Spy labels~\citep{glitchzoo2021},
dual-detector noise coincidence FAR measurement, and follow-up at published
GWTC/cWB GPS times from open catalogs. The catalog analysis is a consistency
check, not a discovery claim.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Unknown-morphology efficiency}
Figure~\ref{fig:eff} shows Keno residual EP achieving 100\% efficiency from
SNR\,2--12 at 1\% FAR. Raw excess power on unsubtracted strain yields lower
efficiency on the same injections. Mismatched matched filtering remains near
$\sim$20\% and does not exceed 50\%. The BBH-trained ResNet control, evaluated
on these non-BBH bursts, plateaus below saturation, as expected for a
morphology-specific model applied off-template.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering

  \caption{Detection efficiency versus injected SNR at 1\% FAR for unknown
  morphologies. Baselines include raw excess power, mismatched matched
  filtering, and a BBH-trained ResNet control.}
  \label{fig:eff}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Coherent calibration}
Envelope gating removes most dual-detector noise outliers. With 1500 noise
trials (23 envelope-gated), the coherent EP threshold is $173.1$ at empirical
joint FAR $0.07\%$, distinct from the single-detector residual threshold
$\approx 4005$.

\subsection{Gravity Spy glitch taxonomy}
Single-detector subtraction alone does not reliably suppress glitches. On a
curated O3a Gravity Spy subset of 30 triggers spanning six labels, residual
excess power still exceeded threshold for 23 cases ($76.7\%$), whereas the
single-detector suppression rate (raw trigger absent after subtraction) was
0/30. Injected unknown-morphology controls on the same background were
preserved at 40/40 (residual trigger and overlap\,$\ge 0.75$).

Residual survival by Gravity Spy class ($n=5$ per label in this freeze):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Scattered Light} --- lowest survival (20\%). Its longer,
        noise-like morphology is partially absorbed into $\hat{N}$.
  \item \textbf{Blip} --- moderate survival (60\%). Short chirp-like transients
        are partially suppressed but often remain loud in $R$.
  \item \textbf{Tomte} --- high survival (80\%).
  \item \textbf{Koi Fish}, \textbf{Extremely Loud}, \textbf{Whistle} --- 100\%
        survival in this sample. These burst-like classes are retained in $R$
        similarly to astrophysical bursts.
\end{itemize}
Accordingly, production glitch mitigation relies on dual-detector coincidence
and the envelope-timing veto rather than on single-interferometer subtraction
to remove Gravity Spy morphologies.

\subsection{Catalog GPS follow-up}
Production coincidence recovers 9 of 18 dual-detector catalog events
(Figure~\ref{fig:followup}). GW170817 is rejected by the envelope veto despite
high coherent EP, owing to the Livingston glitch. Contaminated recoveries are
excluded from the detection count. Three cWB-only candidates remain 0/3.

\section{Discussion and limitations}

Keno provides a reproducible software framework for unmodeled residual search.
Morphology-specific BBH classifiers and compact-binary matched-filter
pipelines~\citep{nousi2023,koloniari2025} remain appropriate when the target
signal family is known.

Notable limitations of this prototype include:
\begin{itemize}
  \item limited single-detector glitch rejection, particularly for burst-like
        Gravity Spy classes (Koi Fish, Whistle, Extremely Loud); coincidence
        requirements and the envelope veto provide the primary mitigation;
  \item residual over-subtraction if training coverage is insufficient,
        partially addressed by the recovery-error acceptance criterion;
  \item incomplete recovery of some catalog candidates in this configuration;
  \item network-bound latency on first-time GWOSC downloads (1--3 minutes),
        although cached windows are returned quickly;
  \item development by a single author without collaboration-scale detector
        characterization or production operations support.
\end{itemize}

\section{Reproducibility}

Source code, freeze artifacts, and reproduction scripts are public at
\url{https://github.com/PanosM397/Keno}.
Frozen artifacts live under \texttt{docs/freeze/current/} with checkpoint
SHA256 prefix \texttt{55ce7637\ldots} (label \texttt{2026-07-paper-v1}).
Results may be reproduced with:
\begin{verbatim}
cd ml-engine
python -m app.evaluation.aresgw_class --train
python -m app.prove --freeze --freeze-label 2026-07-paper-v1
\end{verbatim}
Interactive inspection uses the Angular frontend with the Express backend
proxying the FastAPI ML engine; see repository READMEs for local setup.

\section{Conclusion}

Generative noise subtraction followed by template-free residual search can
recover unknown-morphology bursts at controlled false-alarm rate in a
reproducible research prototype. Keno implements this workflow as deployable
software---inference API, orchestration layer, and synchronized residual
visualization---intended for audit and extension alongside existing BBH
classifiers, rather than as a finished gravitational-wave search pipeline.
